Space Debris and the Ethics of Responsibility

Erin Tiha, Kashvi Bhatia, Jesse Ramirez, Raul Hernandez, Jenny Zhang, Yuanyuan Ma

Abstract

Space debris has become a major ethical and engineering challenge as Earth's orbital environment grows increasingly congested with defunct satellites and rocket fragments. While space activity continues to expand, current governance has gaps and is often dependent on voluntary compliance. This commentary argues that space debris should be understood not only as a technical issue but also through the lens of engineering ethics. Drawing on current mitigation policies and international regulatory gaps, this paper shows how debris generation threatens satellites, human spaceflight, communication systems, and the long-term sustainability of orbital space. The discussion applies utilitarianism and deontology to evaluate the responsibilities of engineers, governments, and commercial actors. The paper concludes that stronger international enforcement, improved spacecraft design standards, economic accountability mechanisms, and investment in active debris removal are necessary.

There are a few laws and regulations in place for space debris. The first being the US Orbital Debris Mitigation Standard Practices (ODMSP), first established in 2001 and most recently updated in 2019. The goal of the ODMSP is to address the increase in space debris and minimize its growth going forward. Some of the regulations include a limit on how much space debris is allowed, a prohibition on intentional destruction that would create significant debris, and a requirement that stored energy be depleted after use.

The Federal Communications Commission (FCC) adopted the 5-year rule in 2022, requiring that satellites in low orbit be disposed of within five years of post-mission. Outside of the US, the European Space Agency (ESA) adopted its Zero Debris approach in 2023 requiring a greater than 90% probability of successful disposal, prohibiting the release of particles larger than 1mm, and mandating that spacecraft be designed with interfaces for debris removal. The UN Office of Outer Space Affairs (UNOOSA) similarly calls for post-mission disposal and protection of key orbital regions including Low Earth Orbit (LEO) and Geosynchronous Earth Orbit (GEO).

However, these regulations have significant gaps. The UNOOSA guidelines are voluntary and cannot be strictly enforced, there is no penalty for countries that choose not to follow them. Even the ESA's stricter approach lacks consequences for spacecraft that fail to meet requirements. In 2022, only 55% of satellites followed through with orbit clearance recommendations. The rules need to be stronger, and compliance cannot remain optional.

The 2007 Chinese anti-satellite test (ASAT) is one of the most consequential case studies in space debris ethics. China intentionally destroyed its Fengyun-1C weather satellite to demonstrate technological superiority, leaving behind one of the largest debris fields ever created. Because the destruction occurred at high altitude, the fragments will remain in orbit for years. The Russian BLITS satellite was knocked out of trajectory by resulting fragments, and thousands of conjunctions have been recorded since. More importantly, by deliberately creating a hazard affecting global satellite infrastructure, GPS, climate monitoring, financial transactions, communications, China raised a fundamental ethical question: is it justifiable for a single nation to degrade shared orbital infrastructure for nationalistic benefit?

The ASAT incident also exposed the weakness of international space governance. The Outer Space Treaty of 1967 is outdated, lacks precise definitions, and relies on voluntary compliance rather than binding law. These gaps create conditions for future irresponsibility at scale.

Astroscale and ClearSpace represent a different response — companies actively developing debris removal and in-orbit servicing technology. Astroscale's ADRAS-J mission is the world's first attempt to safely approach and extract large debris through proximity operations. ClearSpace-1, commissioned by ESA, is designed to remove the PROBA-1 satellite from orbit, the first mission of its kind. These companies are important not only as technical demonstrations but as ethical ones: they show that engineers and institutions are beginning to accept responsibility for the long-term consequences of space activity. They also reveal the limitation of leaving orbital sustainability to voluntary initiative. Without binding obligations, cleanup remains optional while debris accumulates.

According to NASA, there are millions of pieces of space debris in low Earth orbit, traveling at up to 18,000 miles per hour. At these speeds, even small fragments can severely damage or destroy spacecraft. Much of this debris cannot be tracked, creating risks that engineers cannot fully predict or control. Micrometeoroids and orbital debris (MMOD) are considered the single biggest risk to NASA's human spaceflight programs.

The ethics of space debris are best understood through two frameworks covered in this course. From a utilitarian perspective, space debris threatens infrastructure that billions of people depend on, the overwhelming majority of whom have no role in generating it. The 2007 ASAT test is a clear utilitarian failure: the harms imposed on global space infrastructure vastly outweigh any benefit received by the acting state. Voluntary compliance regimes make this problem structural: operators can externalize costs onto the commons while retaining private benefits. A 2024 NASA cost-benefit analysis found that active debris removal produces benefits far exceeding upfront costs, yet investment remains misaligned with that reality.

From a deontological perspective, engineers designing satellites and space systems have a duty to protect shared resources regardless of outcome. Even if orbital computing or debris-generating activity offers benefits, that does not remove the professional obligation to design responsibly, disclose risks, and plan for decommissioning. Engineers cannot outsource that responsibility to regulators or commercial incentives.

Addressing space debris requires four categories of reform. First, the transition from voluntary guidelines to binding international obligations with enforceable sanctions. A UN-level body modeled on the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change has been proposed as a mechanism to unify standards. Second, market-focused instruments, launch and orbital taxes, debris offset markets, that align private operator incentives with collective orbital health. Third, accelerated investment in active debris removal technology, with standardized qualification frameworks to reduce regulatory uncertainty for commercial providers. Fourth, data sharing and transparency reform, moving away from military-controlled space monitoring toward a system that supports international cooperation.

The orbital environment is a shared commons. Its long-term viability demands that responsibility be collective, legislative, and oriented toward sustainability, not left to voluntary effort and commercial initiative.
