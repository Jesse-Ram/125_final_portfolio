Project Proposal: Space Debris

Erin Tiha, Kashvi Bhatia, Jesse Ramirez, Raul Hernandez, Jenny Zhang, Yuanyuan Ma

The Issue

The issue that our group wants to address is space debris. These are obsolete man-made parts floating around in space, and the amount keeps increasing. It is dangerous because of its speed and risk of collisions with existing satellites. There is also the danger of making space unusable if there is too much debris to carry out space missions or keep satellites in orbit.

This issue is meaningful because it brings into conversation the topic of lifecycle. Engineering products need to be made with their lifecycle in mind. Design to implementation is what engineers usually aim for. But it is also important to consider how the engineering product is disposed of, whether it can be reused or recycled for minimal environmental impact. Because space debris is not disposed of properly, it is causing issues that will now need to be fixed with another engineering innovation.

Who This Project Is For

This project is for current and aspiring engineers who are either building a new product or want to build a new product. A lot of current engineering products don't really have a disposal system. Engineering products are usually designed with efficiency, cost, and safety in mind — not necessarily disposal. With our 3,000-word commentary, we hope to motivate discussion around the product design cycle, especially when it comes to space-related technological innovations.

Implementation Plan

We are producing a 3,000-word commentary on space debris. Group members will research the current status of space debris, existing action plans, and the risks and public implications of continued debris accumulation. The final goal is to have engineers consider product disposal when designing a product and to emphasize the dangers if it is not considered.

01
Background research on current laws, regulations, and the international legal landscape governing space debris.
02
Identify gaps in current regulations and examine where governance fails.
03
Connect regulatory failures to ethical failures using frameworks covered in class.
04
Examine real-world case studies and propose research-backed solutions.

Success Criteria

Our project will be evaluated against four criteria: analytical depth, ethical integration (applying course concepts), relevance (connecting to real-world case studies), and strong research-backed solutions.

Ethical Frameworks

Our project draws on two frameworks. Utilitarianism — because space debris affects the entire global population, the focus must be on maximizing benefit and preventing harm for all of humanity. Deontology — engineers designing space technology have a moral duty not to create harm for humanity. The failure to plan for debris disposal directly violates that duty.

Evaluation

The project will be evaluated by how well it covers the available literature, assesses gaps in regulatory practice, clearly analyzes the situation through ethical frameworks, and produces recommendations grounded in relevant real-world evidence.
