Concept Review

Course Concepts Applied

FEREs · McGinn R8
FERE2 & FERE3 — Well Applied
The author correctly identifies FERE2 (prevent harm) and FERE3 (inform the public) as the most relevant responsibilities, and applies them specifically to software engineers rather than generically. The ChatGPT tumor prognosis example effectively illustrates a concrete FERE2 violation. FERE4 is addressed and appropriately de-prioritized in favor of public safety.
correctly applied
Blameworthiness · van de Poel R6
Four Conditions — Well Applied
The author applies all four conditions of blameworthiness — wrongdoing, causal contribution, foreseeability, and freedom of action — to distribute responsibility between engineers and users. The conclusion that engineers bear more blame is well-reasoned and grounded in the framework rather than asserted without support.
correctly applied
Tripartite Model · van de Poel R6
Role Responsibility — Well Applied
The three-segment model is used effectively to map tech executives, software engineers, and patients to distinct roles with distinct responsibilities. The tension between segment 1 pressure (rapid deployment) and segment 3 harm (patient misdiagnosis) is clearly articulated.
correctly applied
Bateson · R1
Ecological Flexibility — Partially Applied
The author uses Bateson's framework to frame the healthcare system as an ecosystem where LLMs introduce harmful flexibility. The concept is applied correctly in principle, but the connection could be developed further — specifically, what "ecological pathology" looks like in this context and what restoring flexibility would require.
partially developed
Verbeek · CR7
Morality in Design — Well Applied
The reference to Verbeek's argument that artifacts must mediate human behavior is used correctly to critique the hiding of disclaimers in small text. The author correctly identifies this as a design ethics failure, not just a legal one. This is one of the stronger concept applications in the analysis.
correctly applied
Societal Experiment · van de Poel R15
Responsible Experimentation — Applied
The author references the concept of responsible social experimentation when arguing that deploying LLMs without proper regulation violates the conditions for ethical societal experiments. The connection to informed consent and transparency is implicit but could be made more explicit.
could be stronger
Law vs. Ethics Fallacy
Confusion of Law and Ethics, Well Identified
The author correctly names and applies the law/ethics fallacy when critiquing FDA disclaimer loopholes. The argument that legal compliance does not equal ethical conduct is a strong and accurate application of a concept covered in class.
correctly applied
Areas for Development

What Could Be Stronger

01
Technology Transfer / Cultural Context (R13) — The analysis mentions accessibility for marginalized populations but does not engage with the cultural and infrastructural assumptions embedded in LLM design, which McGinn's ERPITT framework would surface. Different healthcare systems and languages represent meaningful ethical variables not addressed.
02
Upstream Acquiescence (R10) — The pressure on software engineers to deploy quickly is identified but not connected to McGinn's upstream acquiescence pattern. Naming that pattern would have strengthened the argument that early ethical compromises accumulate into serious downstream harm.
03
Workplace Culture (R16) — The analysis identifies "technological enthusiasm" as a pressure on engineers but does not use McGinn's workplace culture framework to name the organizational context that makes this pressure systemic rather than individual.
Conclusion

Final Assessment

Anaisha's final analysis demonstrates a solid command of the course's ethical frameworks and applies them with specificity to a genuinely complex case. The strongest elements are the FERE analysis, the blameworthiness framework, and the Verbeek application to UI design. The analysis would benefit from deeper engagement with McGinn's case-based concepts upstream acquiescence, workplace culture, and technology transfer which would elevate it from a strong ethical argument to a fully contextual one. Overall this is a well-executed final analysis.
