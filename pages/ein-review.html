Course Concepts Applied

FEREs · McGinn R8
FERE2 & FERE3 — Well Applied
The author correctly identifies FERE2 (prevent harm) and FERE3 (inform the public) as the most relevant responsibilities, and applies them specifically to software engineers rather than generically. The ChatGPT tumor prognosis example effectively illustrates a concrete FERE2 violation. FERE4 is addressed and appropriately de-prioritized in favor of public safety.
correctly applied
Blameworthiness · van de Poel R6
Four Conditions — Well Applied
The author applies all four conditions of blameworthiness — wrongdoing, causal contribution, foreseeability, and freedom of action — to distribute responsibility between engineers and users. The conclusion that engineers bear more blame is well-reasoned and grounded in the framework rather than asserted without support.
correctly applied
Tripartite Model · van de Poel R6
Role Responsibility — Well Applied
The three-segment model is used effectively to map tech executives, software engineers, and patients to distinct roles with distinct responsibilities. The tension between segment 1 pressure (rapid deployment) and segment 3 harm (patient misdiagnosis) is clearly articulated.
correctly applied

Conclusion

Final Assessment

This review incorporates all 18 examples of ethics concept application found in McGinn's The Ethical Engineer, as required for the EiN Reviewer assignment.

Anaisha's final analysis of AI diagnostic chatbots is a genuinely strong piece of ethical reasoning. Her core argument, that software engineers violate FERE2 and FERE3 by deploying systems without adequate safeguards and by burying disclaimers in fine print, is well-supported and consistently developed. The blameworthiness framework is applied with precision, and the Verbeek reference to UI design as a moral act is a strong argument. Additonally, she mentions how the analysis engages with the full depth of McGinn's ethical framework across his case studies, and where additional concept application would have strengthened it.

The analysis handles the utilitarian counterargument well. By arguing that the total benefit of democratizing medical information cannot justify individual harm, Anaisha mirrors McGinn's critique in the Ford Pinto case (Case 11: Limits of Utilitarianism), where placing a monetary value on human life to avoid a design fix violated the no harm principle regardless of the cost-benefit outcome. She does not cite Ford directly, but her response follow the same ethical logic.

Her treatment of FDA disclaimer loopholes is the strongest section of the paper. The argument that legal compliance does not equal ethical connects will onto McGinn's Google Street View case (Case 17: Legality vs. Ethical Acceptability), where an engineer collected payload data that was legally permissible but ethically irresponsible because it violated the spirit of FERE1. Anaisha names this the law and ethics fallacy and applies it correctly, which demonstrates genuine command of a McGinn concept beyond surface definition.

The pressure Anaisha identifies on software engineers to deploy quickly reflects what McGinn calls the upstream acquiescence pattern (Case 4: Apple Newton MessagePad and Case 8: Space Shuttle Challenger). In both cases, engineers expedited timelines early in a project which caused issues downstream. Her model mentions that in segment one, executives push for rapid release, while in segment three, patients bear the consequences. However, naming the upstream pattern would help her argument.

Anaisha recommends redesigning apps to display uncertainty clearly and restrict high-stakes prompts. This is essentially a call for what McGinn terms Design for Ethical Compatibility (Case 5: Employee Database Management System), the responsibility to ensure the product does not create violations of user values like privacy and informed consent. A related concept, Design for Local Cultural Compatibility (Case 16: KickStart Innovations), would also apply here, since AI diagnostic tools designed for healthcare contexts contain assumptions about literacy, and access that may not hold across different populations. Neither concept is named in the analysis.

The paper briefly acknowledges that AI tools may benefit marginalized populations with limited healthcare access, but does not engage with the risks of deploying those tools into contexts they were not built for. McGinn's Bhopal case (Case 7: Union Carbide Bhopal and Technology Transfer) makes this concrete: Union Carbide moved industrial technology into India without accounting for local infrastructure, training, or safety culture — and the result was catastrophic. The same logic applies to AI diagnostic tools trained on English-language data and Western healthcare assumptions being used in communities where neither holds. A technology only works if the context it enters can support it. That point was missing from the analysis.

The discussion of who is responsible when an AI system causes harm touches on blameworthiness but does not fully engage with the question of where whistleblowing responsibility begins. McGinn's TRW Missile Defense case (Case 13: Whistleblowing Conditions) an enigineer may know that a system is producing unreliable outputs and continues to deploy it, the ethical responsibility to alert the public may shift from permissible to obligatory. Given that Anaisha documents cases where engineers were aware of hallucination rates, this idea would have strengthened her FERE3 argument.

Anaisha's observation that tech executives sidestep responsibility by pointing to legal disclaimers connects to McGinn's analysis of cognitive myopia (Case 1: Cadillac DeVille Engine-Control Chip), when engineers fail to consider the harful consequences of their work because they are focused on the immediate task. This pattern also appears in AI deployment when developers focus on individual user interactions without considering the global consequences on the healthcare system as a whole.

The dual-use dimension of AI diagnostic tools, where the same system that provides medical advice can also harvest sensitive health data, connects to McGinn's Nanotechnology R&D case (Case 10: Macro-ethics and Dual-Use Dilemma). Engineers working on AI have a responsibility to educate themselves on the social consequences of their technology. Particularly when those consequences may harm vulnerable populations who have no role in the design decisions.

The workplace culture dimension is present in Anaisha's analysis through the concept of technological enthusiasm, but it is not developed using McGinn's framework. The Manhattan Westway Project case (Case 15: Workplace Culture and Cultural Conflict) shows how a build it and ship it culture can pressure engineers to prioritize speed over integrity, which is exactly the dynamic Anaisha describes at AI companies.

The Schon Affair (Case 3: Bell Labs Research Misconduct) is relevant here. When AI companies publish accuracy benchmarks without disclosing hallucination rates, they are doing the same thing Schon did: putting out results that misrepresent the actual integrity of the work. Anyone who signs off on that shares in the blame.

McGinn's SDI Battlefield Management Software case (Case 2: Conflict of Interest) adds another dimension that Anaisha's analysis does not reach. When AI companies have commercial incentives tied to maximizing user engagement, those interests may conflict directly with the public interest in accurate medical information. The same rule applies, the client, and society, must take precedence over private financial interests.

The Citicorp Center Tower case (Case 6: Normal vs. Radical Design) connects well to AI diagnostic tools. Using a general large language model to give medical advice without proper medical testing and validation is risky because it treats a new and uncertain technology like it is already proven. McGinn’s point in the Citicorp case is that when engineers work with a more unusual or less tested design, they have a greater responsibility to be careful and check for possible failures.

McGinn's Composite-Material Bicycle case (Case 9: Problem Redefinition) suggests that engineers have a responsibility to redefine the problem when a client's initial request is unsafe. Applied here, software engineers who know that users are asking AI systems questions the systems cannot reliably answer have a duty to redesign the interaction rather than simply adding a disclaimer and proceeding.

The Hyatt Regency Walkway case (Case 14: Design for Manufacturability) shows how a practical design change can become dangerous when engineers do not go back and check whether the system is still safe. In that case, a change made for manufacturing reasons was not properly recalculated, which led to a catastrophic failure. A similar issue can happen with AI if a model is adjusted for a new field but its performance is not carefully rechecked for that setting. The main idea is that even if a change seems practical or efficient, engineers still have a responsibility to make sure it does not create new risks.

Finally, McGinn’s Topf and Sons case (Case 12: Decontextualization) and the Opioid Biosynthesis case (Case 18: Rawls’s Difference Principle) help define the ethical limit of this issue. The Topf case warns against pretending that engineers are only responsible for building a tool and not for how it may be used. The Opioid Biosynthesis case adds that a technology is not ethical if the people most likely to be harmed are also the ones with the fewest alternatives or protections. Applied to AI diagnosis, this means it is not enough for the technology to help some users if it creates the greatest risks for people who are already the most vulnerable.

Taken together, Anaisha's analysis is a strong foundation that engages seriously with FERE2, FERE3, blameworthiness, and the law versus ethics distinction. The analysis would reach a higher level by explicitly naming upstream acquiescence, cognitive myopia, design for ethical compatibility, technology transfer ethics, and the whistleblowing threshold, all of which McGinn's case studies establish as directly applicable to this scenario. The argument is not wrong where it stands. It is simply not yet as deep as the case warrants.
