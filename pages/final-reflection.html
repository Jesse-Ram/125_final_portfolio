Summary Reflection

Ethics, Engineering,
and My Place in the Picture

A reflection on what it means to be an ethical engineer, and how this semester changed how I answer that question.

⟶ See Annexe for extended analysis, 15 concept cards, and EiN cases

At the start of this semester I wrote in my learning proposal that I wanted this course to give me the ability to make logical and ethical choices as an engineer, to recognize when an engineering implication conflicts with my values, and to know what to do about it. This felt like a simple question but to apply these skills in practice would be challenging

The question the course kept returning to, what is the relationship between ethics, engineering, and society?. Through my anlysis in this course I have learned that engineering is never a neutral act. Every system built, every risk accepted, every design decision made in a schedule-pressured meeting shapes who benefits and who bears the cost. McGinn captures the obligation that follows: "concern for the man himself and his fate must always form the chief interest of all technical endeavors — so that the creations of our mind shall be a blessing and not a curse" (R17, p. 310). My place in that picture as a mechanical engineer who now understands that technical competence and ethical responsibility are not separate ideas. One without the other is incomplete.

What It Means to Be an Ethical Engineer

Van de Poel draws a distinction that passive responsibility looks backward, assigning blame after harm has occurred, while active responsibility looks forward, demanding that engineers anticipate harm before it becomes reality (R6, p. 11). Mechanical engineers design systems that operate in the physical world, where failures are not abstract. They are structural collapses, failed joints, pressure releases. preventing harm before it happens is not just an ideal. It is part of an engineers job.

That duty is harder to fulfill than it sounds. McGinn documents the "ethics gap", the disconnect between engineering training and professional reality, as a consequence of engineers moving from cadual owned businesses into large corporate hierarchies (R5, p. 13). The result is a tug of war between employer loyalty and public welfare that most engineers encounter with little time to react. Survey data makes the scale of the problem concrete: 84.2% of practitioners believe students will face significant ethical issues in their careers, yet most programs offer no concrete preparation (R5, p. 5). My interview for the Interview Summary brought this to life directly. He explained that engineers can feel pressure to move quickly even when uncertainty still exists. Instead of ignoring that pressure, he said engineers need to speak up, ask harder questions, and push for more testing when something does not feel fully resolved.

Our group's civic engagement project on space debris made the same point from a different angle. The problem is not that aerospace engineers designed bad hardware. It is that product lifecycle, what happens to a satellite after its operational life ends and how this leave no responsibility for the engineers who built it. Bateson’s idea of ecological flexibility helps explain the problem that when a system is built to focus too much on one goal, it can stop adapting to the bigger environment around it, which can lead to larger problems or “ecological pathologies.” (R1, p. 503). For example, the Group Project pushed me to think about design not as a process that ends at implementation, but one that must account for the full arc of a product's life.

The Skills Ethical Engineering Requires

Three important skills came out of this semester. These are not just personality traits, but skills that can be learned and improved with practice.

The first is ethical imagination. Roeser mention that emotions are not noise in the ethical signal, they are part of it. "Purely rational reflection would not be able to provide us with the imaginary power that we need to envisage future scenarios and to take part in other people's perspectives" (CR4, p. 106). I came to believe that neither side should be deciding the value of the other’s work, because both designers and manufacturers bring different skills and face different challenges. The bigger issue is that fair wages should be set by people in positions of power who have the ability to create better standards for both. This is especially important for workers in third-world factory conditions, who are often left with little choice but to accept unfair pay and poor working conditions.

The second is normative argumentation, the ability to identify what kind of ethical claim you are making and whether it actually holds (R2/R3). Our space debris project relied on both utilitarian and deontological frameworks, and knowing the difference mattered. A utilitarian argument about maximum harm prevention leads to different policy recommendations than a deontological argument about engineers' duties to future generations. The EiN Review was where I practiced this most directly, having to identify not just what went wrong in a case but what category of ethical failure it represented.

The third is communicative responsibility. Tufte’s discussion of the Columbia disaster shows that the way engineers present information can shape whether uncertainty is taken seriously or lost in a polished summary. As information moves through an organization, “key explanations and supporting information are filtered out” (CR5, p. 5). For mechanical engineers, that matters a lot. Things like stress test data, safety margins, and failure mode analyses usually pass through several layers before they reach the people making final decisions. That is why Tufte’s point stayed with me: “making an evidence presentation is a moral act as well as an intellectual activity” (CR5, p. 7).

These skills do not belong in a single standalone ethics course. They belong in design studios, technical writing classes, and capstone projects, wherever engineers first encounter the decisions that will define their careers. Pawley's research shows that the way engineering faculty define their discipline functions as a "boundary-drawing" mechanism, determining which problems count as engineering problems and who belongs in the field (CR2, p. 309). A mechanical engineering curriculum that treats lifecycle, safety communication, and stakeholder analysis as elective concerns will produce engineers who are technically capable and ethically underprepared, which is exactly the gap McGinn describes (R5, p. 5).

Where I Go From Here

This semester helped me see that ethics, engineering, and society are all connected. As a mechanical engineer, I see my role as not just designing systems that work, but also thinking about who is affected by them,and how those risks are communicated. One thing I want to carry forward is the habit of questioning assumptions instead of accepting them too quickly. My interview with Nico and our space debris project both helped me grow in that area because they showed me that good engineering is not only about performance, but also about responsibility. Going forward, I want to be the kind of engineer who takes both the technical and human side of a problem seriously.

McGinn's foundational-contextual approach asks engineers to hold technical expertise and ethical responsibility simultaneously, attending to the political, cultural, and sociological context of their work at all times (R17, p. 307). For a mechanical engineer, that means treating every design decision as a claim about the world. For instance, who this system is for, what it assumes about its users, what happens when it fails, and who bears the cost when it does. This portfolio is the beginning of that practice, and not the completion of it.

"The creations of our mind shall be a blessing and not a curse to mankind." — McGinn, R17, p. 310
