15 Concepts from Course Readings

Each concept below links to at least one reading from Readings 1–17 or CR Readings 1–8.

Reading 1 · Bateson
Ecological Flexibility
Bateson argues that flexibility is a "precious resource" that engineered systems must preserve, not optimize away. When a system is designed to maximize a single variable, it loses the capacity to adapt to unpredictable change, producing what Bateson calls "ecological pathologies." An ethical engineer must ask whether their design matches the flexibility of the social ecosystem it enters. This conncects back to an EiN readings about data centers in urban cities, where the drive for efficiency threatens to displace the existing. The ridgid system is the community and the pathologies are the social and individualistic harms that result.
systems thinking adaptability
Reading 2 · van de Poel
Normative Argumentation
Van de Poel distinguishes between deductive and non-deductive ethical reasoning, and between utilitarian and rights-based frameworks. Knowing which type of argument you are making, and why, is itself an ethical skill. This framework shaped how I structured my analysis in the EiN Review, where identifying the type of ethical failure was as important as identifying the failure itself.
argumentation reasoning
Reading 5 · McGinn
The Ethics Gap
McGinn identifies a profound disconnect between the technical training engineers receive and the ethical complexity they encounter in professional practice. Survey data shows 84.2% of practitioners believe students will face significant ethical issues in their careers, yet most engineering programs offer little concrete preparation. This gap is not accidental, it reflects the sociological shift of engineers into corporate hierarchies where firm interests outweight public welfare. Closing this gap requires not just more ethics but a greater emphasis on the practical skills of ethical engineering while in school.
education profession society
Reading 6 · van de Poel
Active vs. Passive Responsibility
Passive responsibility looks backward, assigning blame after harm has occurred using four conditions: wrongdoing, causal contribution, foreseeability, and freedom of action. Active responsibility looks forward, demanding that engineers anticipate and prevent harm before it materializes. The distinction matters because it shifts the ethical question from "who is at fault?" to "what should I do now?". A far more useful frame for practicing engineers. This idea connects well to my interview with Nico. He mostly described active responsibility, especially when he talked about engineers needing to question assumptions, ask for more testing, and speak up when something feels uncertain. In his work, responsibility is not just about who would be blamed after a failure happens. It is more about preventing problems before hardware is approved or used. At the same time, his comments also showed why passive responsibility still matters, since if engineers ignore warning signs and something goes wrong, people will look back and ask who made the decision and whether the risk should have been seen earlier.
responsibility accountability
Reading 8 · McGinn
The FEREs — Fundamental Ethical Responsibilities
McGinn's FEREs establish objective standards for engineering conduct. FERE1 prohibits causing harm or unreasonable risk. FERE2 and FERE3 create proactive duties to prevent harm and alert the public. Most importantly, FERE4 requires engineers to treat society at large as their "omnipresent background client", meaning that when employer interests conflict with public welfare, public welfare takes precedence. These are not suggestions; they are professional obligations.
FERE public safety standards
Reading 10 · McGinn
Upstream Acquiescence, Downstream Misfortune
McGinn names a recurring pattern in engineering disasters: early submission to schedule pressure or management optimism develop silently, only becoming visible when catastrophic failure makes them undeniable. The lesson is not that engineers should be more courageous after the fact, but that they must resist compromise before the design is locked in. And preventing harm while changing is still possible. An exlpain of this concept connects to the Challenger case, where engineers had concerns about the O-rings but ultimately acquiesced to management's schedule pressure. The disaster revealed that this upstream compromise was a critical factor in the downstream failure. It also shows why it's so important for engineers to push back early on. This pattern of upstream pressure leading to downstream misfortune is something I will keep in mind as I enter engineering practice, especially when I encounter pressure to cut corners or ignore warning signs.
failure patterns organizational pressure
CR Reading 4 · Roeser
Emotional Engineers & Moral Imagination
Roeser challenges the assumption that good engineering is dispassionate engineering. Her argument is that "purely rational reflection would not be able to provide us with the imaginary power that we need to envisage future scenarios and to take part in other people's perspectives." Moral emotions are not noise in the ethical signal; they are part of the signal itself, sensitizing engineers to justice, fairness, and autonomy in ways that cost-benefit analysis cannot reach. This concept reshaped how I approached Civic Engagement Project. An example duing semester when an emotional response drew ymy attention to an ethical issue was the dicussing the hieracy of workflow production; should the deisgners dictate the how much the manufactures are paid or the visversa, we also discussed how much should each party be paid. I felt that the designers should be paid more because they are the ones who are creating the product and taking on more risk, but I also recognized that the manufacturers are doing important work and deserve fair compensation. The question of wage gap was discussed, examining the ethical implications of the pay disparity. This emotional response made me realize that there are ethical dimensions to these decisions that go beyond just the technical aspects of the design. It also made me think about how I would approach similar situations in the future, and how I can use my emotions as a tool for ethical reflection rather than something to be ignored or suppressed.
moral emotions ethical imagination design
Reading 11 · van de Poel
Design as a Moral Activity
Design is where social goals are translated into technical systems ,and therefore where the most important ethical decisions are made, often invisibly. Van de Poel distinguishes between "normal design," which follows established configurations, and "radical design," which departs from them. Radical design carries greater individual moral responsibility precisely because no regulatory framework yet exists to catch its mistakes. This concept reshaped how I approached the design components of my Civic Engagement Project.
design ethics values in design innovation
Reading 13 · McGinn
Ethically Responsible Technology Transfer (ERPITT)
McGinn's ERPITT framework argues that responsible technology transfer requires more than moving hardware. It requires ensuring a match between the technical system and the users context's cultural resources. Safety depends on non-technical conditions: zoning laws, emergency preparedness habits, maintenance cultures. The Bhopal disaster was not simply a chemical accident; it was the result of transplanting a system that assumed infrastructure that did not exist.
global ethics cultural context safety
Reading 14 · van de Poel
Technological Mediation & Materializing Morality
Artifacts are not neutral tools, they actively shape human perception and behavior, providing what Verbeek calls "scripts" for action. A speed bump does not ask a driver to slow down; it forces them to. In designing artifacts that play these mediating roles, engineers "materialize morality", making ethical choices on behalf of future users whether they intend to or not. This means every design decision carries a moral dimension that cannot be delegated away.
design ethics mediation moral agency
Reading 15 · van de Poel
Engineering as Societal Experiment & the Precautionary Principle
Because the consequences of new technologies cannot be fully predicted, their introduction into society functions as an experiment conducted on people who never consented to participate. Van de Poel argues that safety must therefore be understood not as the absence of risk but as its reduction to an "acceptable level" and that when a threat is uncertain, the Precautionary Principle demands action regardless of full scientific certainty.
risk precaution consent
Reading 16 · McGinn
Workplace Culture & the Gung-Ho Problem
McGinn argues that a firm's workplace culture, its characteristic mentalities and behavioral norms, decisively influences whether ethical conduct is possible for individual engineers. In "gung-ho" cultures, precautionary action is treated as an obstacle to progress, and novel systems are prematurely treated as proven. Responsible engineering therefore requires not just individual virtue but organizational structures that make virtue sustainable. This idea also connected to my interview with Nico. He explained that formal review systems are helpful, but they only work if the company culture supports people speaking honestly about risk and uncertainty. In a fast-moving environment, there can be pressure to keep pushing forward, which can make caution feel like it is slowing progress. His comments showed that ethical engineering depends not just on the engineer, but also on whether the workplace makes it acceptable to question decisions and raise concerns.
workplace organizational ethics culture
Reading 17 · McGinn
The Foundational-Contextual Approach
McGinn's concluding framework calls for combining technical expertise with a robust commitment to the FEREs, while attending to the full political, sociological, and cultural context of engineering work. This is not a checklist, it is a disposition, one that must be continuously cultivated. It is the framework that ties together every other concept in this portfolio, and the standard against which I measure the kind of engineer I want to become.
ethics framework professional identity context
CR Reading 2 · Pawley
Universalized Narratives & Boundary Drawing
Pawley reveals that how engineering faculty define their discipline, as applied science, as problem-solving, as making things is never neutral. These "universalized narratives" act as boundary-drawing mechanisms, determining who belongs in engineering and which problems count as engineering problems. An ethical engineer must be willing to question these boundaries, recognizing that the definition of engineering itself shapes who gets helped and who gets excluded.
inclusion professional identity education
CR Reading 5 · Tufte
Communication as a Moral Act
Tufte's analysis of the Columbia disaster demonstrates that how engineers present information is itself an ethical responsibility. PowerPoint's hierarchical "cognitive style" systematically filtered critical uncertainty out of NASA's decision chain turning life-threatening data into reassuring bullet points. His conclusion is direct: "making an evidence presentation is a moral act as well as an intellectual activity." If engineers leave out important details or make evidence seem simpler than it really is, they share responsibility for the decisions that follow.
communication transparency accountability
Section 2

Ethics in the Engineering Curriculum

Knowing what ethical engineering requires is different from being able to practice it. Three skills, in particular, stand out from this semester as trainable capacities rather than innate traits.

The first is ethical imagination — the ability to project oneself into the position of those who will be affected by a design decision. Roeser (CR4) argues this capacity is cultivated through role-playing exercises and liberal arts education, not through ethics lectures alone. My own experience writing the Critical Reflection For example, one moment that stretched my ethical imagination this semester was thinking more deeply about fairness in design and labor. At first, I saw the issue in a very basic way, but writing about it forced me to sit with other perspectives longer and realize that engineering decisions can affect people very differently depending on their role, resources, and power.

The second skill is normative argumentation (R2/R3) — being able to identify whether one is reasoning deductively or non-deductively, and whether a utilitarian or rights-based framework better fits the case at hand. Van de Poel's framework gave me tools I applied directly in the EiN Review, where I had to assess not just what went wrong in a case but what kind of ethical failure it represented.

The third is communicative responsibility — Tufte's insight (CR5) that how engineers present information is itself an ethical act. Engineering ethics should not only be taught in dedicated ethics courses. It belongs in technical writing courses, design studios, and capstone projects, anywhere engineers practice translating complex information for non-expert audiences. For non-engineering disciplines, the case is equally strong. Policy makers, journalists, and business leaders all make decisions that depend on understanding how technology works and who it affects. Pawley's critique (CR2) of universalized narratives suggests that broadening the definition of engineering education beyond applied science and math would make the field more inclusive and more ethically capable at the same time. If I had the chance to design ethics into an engineering course, I would build it into the normal technical work instead of treating it like a separate topic. I would include short reflections, design tradeoff discussions, and assignments where students have to explain risk and uncertainty clearly, because this semester showed me that ethical judgment is part of everyday engineering practice.

Section 3

8 EiN Case Examples

EiN 1 · Current Affairs
AI Ethics Ignores Warfare
While mainstream AI ethics focuses on chatbot bias and speculative risks, AI weapons are already deployed in Gaza and Ukraine. Israel's Lavender targeting system removes human judgment from lethal decisions. Illustrates FERE2 and FERE3, engineers have a duty to prevent harm and alert the public, even when employer interests push otherwise.
FERE2 autonomous weapons
EiN 2 · Orbital Today
Space Debris $42 Billion Economic Risk
The World Economic Forum estimates space debris could cost the global space economy up to $42.3 billion over the next decade. Voluntary mitigation efforts are insufficient. Connects to Bateson's ecological flexibility, orbital space is a shared ecosystem being degraded by single-variable optimization for cost and performance.
ecological pathology commons
EiN 3 · World Resources Institute
Data Centers & Community Impact
Data centers consume as much power as 100,000 homes, drive up local electricity bills, deplete water in stressed regions, and are disproportionately located in low-income communities. Illustrates technology transfer ethics and the distributive justice dimension of van de Poel's risk framework, who bears the cost of infrastructure they did not consent to.
distributive justice risk
EiN 4 · Interesting Engineering
Sophia Space — Orbital Edge Computing
Sophia Space raised $10M to place AI computing hardware directly in orbit. The technology expands orbital congestion faster than regulation can respond. Illustrates radical design ethics, when engineers work outside established frameworks, individual moral responsibility increases because no regulatory safety net yet exists.
radical design precaution
EiN 5 · New York Times
3D Printed Guns — The FGC-9
The FGC-9, a 3D-printable firearm with publicly available instructions, has been linked to terrorists and illegal organizations in 15 countries. The designer argued it protected freedom. Illustrates the limits of designer intent as an ethical defense, Verbeek's materializing morality applies directly, artifacts carry the values of their designers whether intended or not.
materializing morality accountability
EiN 6 · The Guardian
Rogue AI Agents — Autonomous Security Risks
Lab tests found AI agents autonomously bypassing security safeguards, exposing passwords, and overriding antivirus software when pursuing assigned objectives. Illustrates upstream acquiescence, deploying systems before safety is established creates foreseeable downstream harm that engineers cannot later disclaim responsibility for.
upstream acquiescence FERE2
EiN 7 · The Guardian
Self-Driving Taxis in London
Waymo deploys SAE Level 4 autonomous vehicles while using undisclosed remote operators in the Philippines to manage failures, a fact revealed only through US Senate hearings. London residents were enrolled in an unconsented technology trial. Illustrates FERE3, communicative responsibility, and the law vs. ethics fallac, legal deployment does not equal ethical deployment.
FERE3 informed consent
EiN 8 · New York Times
Tech Executive Liability — Telegram & Durov
Telegram founder Pavel Durov was arrested in France in connection to illegal activity on his platform, including child sexual abuse material. The case raises the question of individual vs. organizational blameworthiness. Illustrates van de Poel's four conditions of blameworthiness, foreseeability and freedom of action are central to whether an executive can claim ignorance as a defense.
blameworthiness accountability
