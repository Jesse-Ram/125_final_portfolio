Interview Summary

Summary and analysis of my interview with a practicing engineer on ethics in professional life.

On February 18, 2026, I interviewed Nico Montoya, a founding engineer at Orbital Operations, during a phone call. I chose to interview Nico because his work is closely connected to the career path I hope to pursue in aerospace propulsion and launch vehicle development. I first connected with him because he had previously worked at Stoke Space, a company I interviewed with, and we stayed in contact afterward. Over time, he has offered me guidance on propulsion design, testing philosophy, and early career decisions in aerospace. Nico also has experience at major organizations such as NASA, Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman, SpaceX, and Blue Origin, which gave him a broad perspective on engineering decisions across both established and fast-moving aerospace environments. Because his work has involved designing technology in high-stakes defense-related settings, I felt he would offer especially valuable insight into the ethical responsibilities engineers face in industry.

Nico currently works as a founding engineer at Orbital Operations, which focuses on space vehicles used for national security applications. In this role, he does a wide range of work across engineering processes, from early design decisions to testing, troubleshooting, and supporting hardware integration. Compared to positions at larger companies, his role requires him to help wherever needed as the company grows. This gave me insight into how engineers in startup environments must constantly balance technical goals with practical limits such as time, resources, and risk.

One of the most important things I learned from Nico was that ethical issues in engineering are often not dramatic or obvious, but they still carry serious consequences. He described a challenge in aerospace engineering that comes from overreliance on analysis. A design may look correct in CAD, simulation, or internal calculations, but real hardware can behave differently once it is manufactured, assembled, and tested. Moving forward with too much confidence in incomplete analysis becomes an ethical issue, not just a technical mistake.

When I asked about workplace resources for dealing with ethical issues, Nico mentioned several systems that help reduce both technical and ethical risk. These include formal design reviews, test readiness reviews, peer feedback, and documentation practices. These resources matter because they create checkpoints where assumptions can be questioned and concerns can be raised before a design moves forward. Instead of leaving major decisions to one person alone, the process encourages shared responsibility and collective accountability.

At the same time, Nico noted that these formal processes are only as effective as the culture around them. Engineers must feel comfortable being honest about uncertainty, asking difficult questions, and challenging decisions when needed. If people are afraid to speak up, then even strong review systems lose much of their value. Based on the interview, I think the resources available in his workplace are solid and well structured, but they could be improved with more direct discussion about assumption validation and accountability during reviews rather than treating them only as technical checkpoints. Companies could also create stronger ways to encourage respectful dissent so that concerns are not overlooked under schedule pressure. Another possible improvement would be an anonymous way for engineers to raise concerns, so individuals do not fear retaliation from higher levels of the company.

Overall, this interview changed how I think about engineering ethics. Before this conversation, I mostly thought of ethics as avoiding obvious wrongdoing, such as lying, hiding information, or breaking rules. After speaking with Nico, I now see that ethics is deeply connected to everyday engineering judgment. Engineers make ethical choices whenever they decide whether a design has been sufficiently tested or whether pressure from stakeholders is outweighing safety and reliability. My biggest takeaway is that ethics in engineering is often about judgment under uncertainty. It is not only about avoiding dishonesty, but about being responsible enough to admit what is still unknown and making decisions that protect safety, reliability, and the people affected by the technology.
