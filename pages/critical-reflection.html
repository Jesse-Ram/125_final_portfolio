The central argument supported in “unicorns,janitors, Ninjas, wizards, and rock stars,” is that dominant narratives about data science, particularly the emphasis on individual “genius” and technical mastery, obscure the collaborative, political, and pluralistic processes that actually produce meaningful and just data work. The authors highlight that shifting “data for good” to “data for co-liberation” requires not just better technical practice but rather reconfiguring power, ownership, and knowledge production. The authors promote a causal and normative (means-end) argument. The implicit goal is equitable and socially just data practices. The author’s key premise is that media and institutional narratives frame data scientist as exceptional individuals, “unicorns” and “wizards.” This framing centers technical expertise while marginalizing community knowledge, and data projects shaped by such hierarchies often reproduce existing power structures. They conclude that just data practice requires pluralism, community ownership, and shared governance structures. (p. 140) reinforces this claim by contrasting “data for good” with “data for co-liberation,” showing that differences lie not just in leadership but also in ownership and infrastructure. Additionally, the authors do not rely solely on critique. They provide counterexamples such as the anti eviction mapping project and the somerville data mural (pp. 142-143), demonstrating how these ideas can function in practice. These examples support the causal claim that reorganizing authority changes both process and outcome. Furthermore, the discussion of “strangers in a dataset” (p. 130–133) illustrates how technical cleaning techniques can eliminate important context, supporting the more general claim that seemingly neutral technical processes can produce epistemic exclusion. The argument is predicated on the idea that diverse governance structures will consistently result in more equitable outcomes, but the text leaves out important trade-offs like conflict, inefficiency, or fragmentation. Clarifying how pluralism handles disagreement without paralysis, and recognizing circumstances in which centralized coordination might be required, would strengthen the case and make the argument more practically grounded. This reading made me think about how I’ve been trained to approach problems as an engineer. Most of the time, the focus is on getting the problem completed in the fastest time, making the system efficient, and improving performance. I’ve rarely stopped to think about who helped define the problem in the first place. The authors’ argument made me realize that technical correctness doesn’t automatically mean the outcome is fair or inclusive. That’s uncomfortable, because I take pride in being precise and analytical. But it also makes sense. If the people affected by a system aren’t involved in shaping it, then even a well designed solution can miss something important. For me, this shifts how I think about responsibility; it’s not just about building something that works, but about being more aware of the context and the people around it.
