Reflection

During this project, I learned that space debris is not only a technical issue but also an ethical and governance problem. Current orbital congestion and weak international law have created real risks for satellites, astronauts, and the broader infrastructure that much of modern life depends on. What surprised me most was how clearly the course concepts mapped onto a real engineering problem, particularly the idea that engineers have active responsibilities that extend beyond design and deployment to the full lifecycle of what they build.

Working on the case studies with Kashvi reinforced this for me directly. Researching Astroscale and ClearSpace showed that some engineers and companies are beginning to accept responsibility for debris that is already in orbit, not just limiting future debris. That shift from mitigation to remediation felt like a practical application of van de Poel's active responsibility framework, and it changed how I think about what engineering accountability actually looks like in practice.

Looking back, I could have improved my contribution by making myself more available for in-person discussion rather than meeting only online. Some of the stronger analytical connections between sections might have emerged more naturally through face-to-face conversation. That is a habit I want to carry forward into future group work.

Work Split Explanation

I scored everyone equally because the project was divided into distinct areas where each member contributed something necessary to the whole. All sections depended on each other, the case studies required the regulatory context, the ethical analysis required the risk data, and the recommendations required all of the above. Because no section could stand alone, it would not be accurate to weight any one contribution above another. Everyone added value to a specific part of the project, and the final result reflected that collective effort.
